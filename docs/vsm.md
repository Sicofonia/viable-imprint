# Viable System Model — Non-Fiction Publishing House (Micro-Enterprise)

**Reference framework:** Viable System Model (VSM) by Stafford Beer
**Organisation modelled:** Non-fiction and popular publishing house, 2–5 people
**Purpose of this document:** Describe the responsibilities, tasks and metrics of each system for use as a basis for delegation and coordination (e.g. Claude Cowork)

---

## Organisational Context

The press specialises in recovering and publishing travel and exploration narratives set in Asia and Central Asia, written by explorers, travellers and adventurers of the 19th and early 20th centuries whose works have entered the public domain. The catalogue includes authors such as Sven Hedin, Aurel Stein, Paul Pelliot, Piotr Kozlov, Mildred Cable, Susie Carson Rijnhart, Alexander Burnes, William Seabrook and Armin Vámbéry, among others.

By working exclusively with public-domain texts, the imprint eliminates rights negotiation but takes on a distinct editorial workload: locating and obtaining reliable sources, assessing the condition of the original text, translating or revising existing translations, and building the critical apparatus (introduction, notes, maps, glossaries).

It operates with a minimal team in which roles overlap. The viability of the system depends on maintaining three simultaneous flows: **text selection and preparation → editorial production → marketing and distribution**.

---

## System 1 — Primary Operations
*The units that create value directly*

### Description

System 1 comprises the activities that form the core business: the processes that transform a manuscript into a published book and place it in the hands of readers. In a micro-enterprise publisher, these operational units are few but distinguishable.

### Operational Units

#### 1A — Selection and Preparation of Public-Domain Texts

**Responsible:** Editorial Director, with support from external researchers or advisors when necessary

**Responsibilities:**
- Identify candidate texts within the corpus of travel and exploration literature on Asia and Central Asia (19th–20th centuries)
- Verify the legal status of each text: confirm that copyright has expired in the jurisdictions relevant to the imprint's distribution
- Locate the best available source of the original text (first edition, editions revised by the author, digitised archives on HathiTrust, Internet Archive, Project Gutenberg, national libraries, etc.)
- Assess the quality and completeness of the text found: gaps, historical typographical errors, recoverable original illustrations or maps
- Decide whether the title will be published in its original language (English, German, French, Russian, Swedish…) or requires translation, and into which language(s)
- Commission or revise the translation if the text does not exist in the imprint's publication language
- Define the critical apparatus for each title: contextual introduction, footnotes, glossary of geographical or cultural terms, historical or newly produced maps, author chronology, secondary bibliography
- Establish the editorial approach for each title: published as a pure historical document? with a contemporary critical reading? aimed at a general or specialist reader?

**Recurring tasks:**
- Periodic review of public-domain digital sources for new finds or recent digitisations
- Maintenance of a candidates database (author, title, year, language, text condition, availability of illustrations, editorial priority)
- Commissioning and monitoring of introductions from external specialists (historians, geographers, Arabists, Sinologists, etc.)
- Pipeline updates: titles under evaluation, in preparation, in production

**Key metrics:**
- Number of candidate titles identified per quarter
- Average time from text identification to delivery of file ready for editorial production
- Percentage of titles with complete critical apparatus (introduction + notes + maps) vs. text-only editions
- Quality of sources used (qualitative assessment: was the best available edition used?)

---

#### 1B — Editorial Production

**Responsible:** Content editor + copyeditor (may be freelance)

**Responsibilities:**
- Content editing (structure, clarity, argumentative coherence)
- Copyediting and style correction
- Coordination with design for interior layout
- Proof review (first and second proof)
- Management of index, bibliography, credits and legal notices

**Recurring tasks:**
- Reading and editorial marking of manuscripts
- Communication of editorial notes
- Version control of the manuscript (v1, v2… final)
- Delivery of file ready for layout

**Key metrics:**
- Average editing time per title (in weeks)
- Number of correction rounds per title
- Percentage of errors detected in proofs vs. post-publication
- Adherence to the production schedule (% titles published on time)

---

#### 1C — Design and Graphic Production

**Responsible:** Designer (may be freelance or shared with another publisher)

**Responsibilities:**
- Cover design (concept, final artwork)
- Interior layout (typography, styles, images, tables)
- Preparation of files for print (PDF/X) and digital formats (ePub, Mobi)
- Maintenance of the imprint's visual identity

**Recurring tasks:**
- Receipt of editorial brief for each title
- Presentation of cover options (2–3 proposals)
- Corrections following internal review
- Delivery of final files to printer and digital platforms

**Key metrics:**
- Number of cover revisions until approval
- Layout time per title
- Technical incidents at printer or on digital platforms (ideally 0)
- Visual coherence of the catalogue (periodic qualitative assessment)

---

#### 1D — Publication and Marketing (Print-on-Demand)

**Responsible:** Editorial Director

**Responsibilities:**
- Upload final files (interior PDF/X and cover image) to the print-on-demand portal (e.g. KDP, IngramSpark, or equivalent)
- Configure metadata for each title on the portal: title, author, ISBN, description, categories, price
- Manage the sales channels available through the portal (Amazon, extended distribution to bookshops, etc.)
- Publish the digital format (ePub) on the relevant platforms
- Communication and marketing for each title (social media, newsletter, specialist communities)
- Liaison with reviewers and specialist media in history, geography and travel

**Recurring tasks:**
- Preparation and upload of files to the portal on the planned launch date
- Verification of the digital proof before activating sales (layout review in the portal viewer)
- Publication on social media and newsletter around the launch
- Monthly review of the sales and royalties dashboard on the portal
- Update of prices or metadata as necessary

**Key metrics:**
- Units sold per title (first 3 months / first year)
- Ratio of physical (print-on-demand) vs. digital sales
- Monthly royalty income on the portal
- Newsletter and social media conversion rate
- Coverage in specialist media or communities (reviews, mentions)

## System 2 — Coordination
*Mechanisms that prevent conflicts and synchronise operations*

### Description

In a micro-enterprise, System 2 is critical because the same people participate in several units of System 1. Without explicit coordination, bottlenecks are inevitable (e.g. design blocked because editorial has not delivered the final original).

### Responsibilities
- Maintain a shared master editorial calendar with all titles in progress, their phases and deadlines
- Manage dependencies between units (production cannot start without editorial; distribution cannot announce without a confirmed print date)
- Resolve priority conflicts when several titles compete for the same resources (designer's time, editor's attention)
- Ensure information flows between units without loss (the designer needs to know the tone of the book before designing the cover)

### Tools and Mechanisms
- Weekly team meeting of 30–45 minutes to review the status of all titles in production
- Kanban board or equivalent (Notion, Trello, Airtable) with columns by editorial phase
- Standardised templates: cover brief, title sheet for distributor, print delivery checklist
- Asynchronous communication channel (e.g. Slack or equivalent) differentiated by project

### Key Metrics
- Number of titles in simultaneous production (optimum: 2–4 for a micro-enterprise)
- Percentage of milestones met on time in the master calendar
- Number of dependency blockages detected and resolution time

## System 3 — Operational Control and Identity of the Present
*Internal management: optimisation, resources and performance*

### Description

System 3 is the day-to-day management. It ensures that System 1 is functioning well in the present: it allocates resources, makes operational decisions, negotiates with the units and guarantees that the company performs as it should. In a micro-enterprise, System 3 typically resides with the Editorial Director.

### Responsibilities
- **Resource allocation:** Decide which title receives more editorial attention at any given time; hire freelancers when volume requires it
- **Internal negotiation:** Balance the demands of the operational units (e.g. acquisition wants to take on more titles, but production is at capacity)
- **Performance monitoring:** Review metrics from each unit and detect deviations from the plan
- **Operational financial management:** Track the annual budget and compile invoices for the annual tax return
- **Operational decision-making:** Print runs, sale prices, discount policy, decision to reprint

### System 3* — Sporadic Audit

The VSM includes an "audit" channel (3*) that allows System 3 to verify operational reality directly without relying solely on reports. In the publishing house this translates to:
- Direct review of the pipeline on the Kanban board (without intermediaries)
- Occasional reading of manuscripts in progress to calibrate real editorial quality
- Direct conversations to sense the quality of key relationships

### Key Metrics
- Gross margin per title (royalty income − production cost)
- Average production cost per title (design, translation if applicable, ISBN)
- Average time of the complete cycle: text identified → book published on portal
- Deviation from the annual budget

## System 4 — Strategic Intelligence and Identity of the Future
*Adaptation: environmental scanning and strategic planning*

### Description

System 4 deals with the future and the environment. While System 3 optimises the present, System 4 asks: what is changing out there? How must we adapt? It is the function of exploration and organisational learning. In a micro-enterprise publisher, this function is the easiest to neglect (there is always something urgent from System 1 absorbing attention), but it is the one that guarantees long-term survival.

### Responsibilities
- **Editorial market surveillance:** What topics are emerging in non-fiction? What are similar publishers publishing? What is receiving media attention?
- **Tracking cultural and academic trends:** In a specialist imprint, detecting which areas of knowledge are attracting growing interest (AI, neuroscience, environmental history, etc.)
- **Exploring new formats and channels:** Audiobooks, courses associated with books, special editions, international licences, co-editions
- **Ecosystem relationships:** Universities, research centres, literary agents, other publishers, festivals and fairs (Frankfurt, Liber, Guadalajara)
- **Catalogue planning 1–3 years ahead:** Which thematic areas to develop, which "collections" to create, which authors to cultivate
- **Assessment of external risks:** Changes in distribution, market concentration, digitalisation, generative AI and its impact on publishing

### Tools and Practices
- Monthly review of cultural and specialist press
- Annual attendance at at least one international fair
- Network of external readers or informal advisory board (researchers, journalists, educators)
- "Catalogue strategy" document revised annually

### Key Metrics
- Number of new editorial opportunities identified per quarter
- Percentage of catalogue in thematic areas with growing demand
- Number of active relationships with potential agents or authors
- Time devoted weekly to System 4 activities (target: minimum 2–3 hours/week for the director)

## System 5 — Identity, Values and Policy
*The purpose: who we are and why we exist*

### Description

System 5 defines the identity of the imprint, its editorial values and its governing principles. It arbitrates conflicts between System 3 (operational efficiency of the present) and System 4 (adaptation to the future) when their demands are contradictory. For this imprint, System 5 has a particularly concrete expression: there is an explicit editorial policy that precisely delimits what the imprint publishes and what it does not, and which serves as the primary filter for all decisions in System 1A.

### Mission

Recover and bring back into circulation the accounts of travellers and explorers who crossed Asia and Central Asia during the 19th and early 20th centuries, restoring the attention they deserve as historical, geographical and human documents of the first order.

### Editorial Policy (Constitutive Criteria)

**Thematic scope — what this imprint publishes:**
- Travel narratives, exploration memoirs and expedition journals written by travellers who crossed Asia and Central Asia (Silk Road, Pamir, Tibet, Xinjiang, Persia, Mongolia, the Hindu Kush, Afghanistan, etc.)
- Historical period: principally the 19th century and the first decades of the 20th
- Reference authors in the catalogue: Sven Hedin, Aurel Stein, Paul Pelliot, Piotr Kozlov, Mildred Cable, Susie Carson Rijnhart, Alexander Burnes, William Seabrook, Armin Vámbéry, and figures from the same corpus
- Exclusively works in the public domain (copyright expired in the imprint's distribution jurisdictions)
- Complementary material also considered: correspondence, expedition reports, articles published in period geographical journals

**Thematic scope — what this imprint does NOT publish:**
- Works still under copyright, regardless of their thematic relevance
- Contemporary travel literature or fiction
- Texts about Asia that lack a first-person exploration or travel component
- Works outside the defined geographical scope (Asia and Central Asia)

**Non-negotiable values:**
- **Source rigour:** always published from the best available edition of the original text; transcriptions of doubtful quality are not accepted
- **Historiographical honesty:** introductions and notes contextualise texts within their era, including their colonial, imperial or ethnocentric biases, without censorship but with critical perspective
- **Quality of the book as object:** design, cartography and critical apparatus are part of the product's value, not optional extras
- **Accessibility:** texts must be readable for a cultured non-specialist reader; notes and glossaries do the necessary mediation work

### Responsibilities of System 5
- Custodian and updater of the editorial policy described above
- Arbitration of borderline cases: does a text fall within the imprint's scope? Is the source found sufficiently reliable? Does the commissioned introduction have the appropriate perspective?
- Decisions on constitutive changes: expanding the geographical scope, incorporating new formats, partnering with other institutions
- Representing the identity of the imprint externally: at fairs, in media, in specialist reader communities

### Practical Expressions of System 5
- Editorial policy document (this text) revised annually
- Explicit evaluation criteria for System 1A when selecting new titles
- Annual strategic review meeting: does the catalogue published this year reflect the mission?
- Protocol for borderline cases: who decides, on what criteria, how the decision is documented

### Qualitative Metrics
*(System 5 is not measured in KPIs; it is evaluated through periodic questions)*

- Does every title published this year fit precisely within the defined editorial policy?
- Do the introductions and critical apparatus meet the standard of historiographical honesty?
- Does our reader community recognise us as a reference for this specific corpus?
- Is there coherence between what we say we are and what we publish?

## Notes for Use in Claude Cowork

Each section of this document can be used as base context for delegating specific tasks. Examples:

**System 1A (Text Selection):** *"You are the editorial director of an imprint specialising in travel and exploration literature on Asia and Central Asia, 19th–20th centuries, working exclusively with public-domain works. Your task is to assess whether the following candidate text meets the criteria of the imprint's editorial policy and what preparatory work it would require…"*

**System 1B (Editorial Production):** *"You are the editor of a publishing house that recovers historical exploration texts from Central Asia. Your task is to review the following transcription of the original text by [author] and identify typographical errors, gaps or inconsistencies with respect to the first edition…"*

**System 4 (Strategic Intelligence):** *"You are the strategy director of a publishing imprint specialising in Sven Hedin, Aurel Stein and their contemporaries. Analyse which titles from the public-domain corpus in this field have the greatest potential interest for today's reader and have not been recently republished…"*

**System 3 (Operational Control):** *"You are the director of a micro-publishing house. You have the following sales and production data. Identify which titles merit a reprint or revised new edition and which should be withdrawn from the active catalogue…"*
